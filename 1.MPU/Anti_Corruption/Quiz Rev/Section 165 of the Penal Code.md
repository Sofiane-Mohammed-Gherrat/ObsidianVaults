
Although we are mainly focusing on the **MACC Act 2009**, your materials mention **Section 165 of the Penal Code** because <mark class="hltr-mygreen2">it is often used in cases involving public servants and "valuable things."</mark>

According to the case study of a policeman in your notes, **Section 165 of the Penal Code** refers to:

> **"A public servant obtaining a valuable thing, without consideration, from a person concerned in any proceeding or business transacted by such public servant."**

### 🔍 Key Breakdown:
*   **Who it applies to:** Specifically **Public Servants**. 🏛️
*   **The Act:** Taking something valuable (money, gifts, property) without paying for it or giving anything of equal value in return ("without consideration").
*   **The Connection:** The person giving the "valuable thing" must be someone who has official dealings or business with that public servant.
*   **The Punishment:** It can carry a jail term of **up to 2 years**, a fine, or both.

### 💡 Example from your notes:
In **Case 1**, a 29-year-old policeman was charged under this section for accepting **RM200** from a couple to avoid bringing them to the station. He was fined **RM1,500** by the court. ⚖️

>Think of **Section 165 of the Penal Code** as a <mark class="hltr-myyellow">rule to stop "thank you gifts"</mark> or "favors" that shouldn't be happening. 

Here is what those specific legal terms actually mean:

### 1. "Obtaining a valuable thing" 💎
This just means the officer takes something that has worth. It doesn’t have to be cash! It could be:
*   A smartphone 📱
*   A luxury watch ⌚
*   Free flight tickets ✈️
*   Even a RM200 "settlement" (like in the case of the policeman in your notes).

### 2. "Without consideration" 💸
In law, "consideration" means **payment or something of equal value**. 
*   **With consideration:** You go to a shop, pay RM5 for a coffee, and get the coffee. You gave "consideration" (money) for it.
*   **Without consideration:** The officer gets the coffee (or RM200) for **free**. They didn't pay for it, and they didn't do a legal job to earn it. It was just handed to them.

### 3. "From a person concerned in any proceeding or business" 🤝
This means the person giving the gift is someone the officer is currently dealing with in their **official job**.
*   **Example:** A contractor giving a free laptop to the officer who is currently deciding which company gets a government building contract.

---

### 📝 Putting it all together:
**The Simplified Version:**
> "A government worker (public servant) takes a free gift or money from someone they are currently doing official work with."

**Why is this a crime?** 
Because even if the officer doesn't *promise* to do anything wrong, taking free things from people you are supposed to be supervising or judging creates a "conflict of interest." It looks like the officer can be "bought."
