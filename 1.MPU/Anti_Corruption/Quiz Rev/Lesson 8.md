# Module 8 – Corporate Liability (MCQ Revision)

## 1️⃣ Corporate Liability – Section 17A MACC Act 2009

### Before 1 June 2020

The MACC Act only applied to **individuals (natural persons)** committing corruption.

### After Section 17A

Now **commercial organisations (legal persons)** can also be punished for corruption offences.

This means:

- Companies
- Partnerships
- LLPs
- Foreign companies

can be **charged if someone associated with them commits bribery**.

---

### Important date

Section 17A enforcement:

📅 **1 June 2020**

Meaning:

- Only corruption **after this date** can be prosecuted.

- The law **has no retrospective effect**.

---

# 2️⃣ Purpose of Section 17A

Section 17A was introduced to comply with international obligations:

1️⃣ **United Nations Convention Against Corruption (UNCAC)**  
2️⃣ **Comprehensive and Progressive Agreement for Trans-Pacific Partnership (CPTPP)**

These require countries to have laws punishing **legal persons (companies)** for corruption.

---

# 3️⃣ Similar Laws in Other Countries

Malaysia modeled Section 17A after:

- **UK Bribery Act 2010 (Section 7)**
- **US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act 1977**


Other countries with similar provisions:

- Brazil
- Canada
- Australia
- India

---

# 4️⃣ What is a “Commercial Organisation”?

A **commercial organisation** refers to **4 categories**.

### Category 1

**Company**
?
Registered under <mark class="hltr-myyellow">Companies Act 2016</mark>

Example:

- AI Engineering Sdn Bhd
- Genting Malaysia Berhad

---

### Category 2

**Partnership**
?
Registered under **Partnership Act 1961**

Characteristics:

- 2–20 partners
- Shared liability

---

### Category 3

**Limited Liability Partnership (LLP)**

Registered under **Limited Liability Partnership Act 2012**

Advantage:

- LLP assets bear the debts.

---

### Category 4

**Foreign companies / partnerships**

That conduct business **in Malaysia**.

Example:

- Multinational companies with Malaysian branches.

---

# 5️⃣ “Person Associated with the Commercial Organisation”

There are **2 categories**.

## Internal persons

- Director
- Partner
- Employee

(Employee applies **regardless of position or status**)

---

## External persons

who :: <mark class="hltr-myyellow">Anyone performing services for the organisation.</mark>

Examples:
?
- Consultants
- Legal firms
- Accounting firms
- Agents
- Middlemen

---

# 6️⃣ When Is an Offence Committed?

> Corporate liability applies when:

A **person associated with the company**:

- **gives**
- **agrees to give**
- **promises**
- **offers**

gratification (bribe) **for the company’s benefit**.

---

### Important exam point

> Corporate liability **does NOT apply** if the person:

- **solicits**
- **accepts**

gratification.

<mark class="hltr-myyellow">Because Section 17A focuses on giving bribes</mark>.

---

# 7️⃣ Purpose of the Bribe

Corporate liability only applies if the bribe is given to:

### 1️⃣ Obtain or retain business

Example:  
Bribing a procurement officer to secure a contract.

---

### 2️⃣ Obtain or retain business advantage

Example:

- Getting permits
- Speeding up payments
- Avoiding legal action

---

### Not corporate liability if:

The bribe is **personal**, for example:

- Avoid traffic fines
- Avoid parking compounds

because it is **not related to company business**.

---

# 8️⃣ Who Can Be Charged?

When corruption occurs:

### The commercial organisation itself

AND

### Individuals involved:

- Director
- Controller
- Officer
- Partner
- Anyone involved in management

can also be charged.

### <mark class="hltr-myyellow">Unless... the individual proves:</mark>

1. The offence was committed without his / her approval or involvement.
2. He / She has taken all appropriate steps to prevent the offence from being committed.


---

# 9️⃣ Punishment

## For Commercial Organisation

Fine:

👉 **Not less than 10× the bribe amount OR RM1 million (whichever higher)**

---

## For Directors / Managers

Penalty:

- **Up to 20 years imprisonment**  
    OR
    
- **Fine ≥ 10× corruption value OR RM1 million**  
    OR
    
- **Both**


---

# 🔟 Defence for Commercial Organisations

The **only defence** is:

The company must prove it had **adequate corruption prevention measures**.

Meaning:

The company tried to **prevent employees or agents from bribery**.

---

# 1️⃣1️⃣ Adequate Procedures Guidelines

Issued by:

📅 **Prime Minister’s Department (Dec 2018)**

Purpose:  
To guide companies on **anti-corruption procedures**.

---

### Important exam concept

There is **NO fixed standard**.

The court determines adequacy based on:

- company size
- business nature
- corruption risk
- organisational complexity

---

# 1️⃣2️⃣ Principle of TRUST

The **5 core principles** for corruption prevention.

### T.R.U.S.T

### T – Top Level Commitment

Company leadership must commit to anti-corruption.

---

### R – Risk Assessment

Company must identify corruption risks.

---

### U – Undertake Control Measures

Examples:

- Anti-corruption policies
- Due diligence checks
- Reporting channels

---

### Due diligence includes

Background checks on:

- contractors
- projects
- employees

---

### Reporting channels must ensure:

- employees can report misconduct
- confidentiality
- action taken on reports

---

### S – Systematic Review, Monitoring & Enforcement

Company must regularly review anti-corruption controls.

---

### T – Training & Communication

Employees must be trained about anti-corruption policies.

---

### Accused

1️⃣ **Pristine Offshore Sdn Bhd** (charged under Section 17A)

2️⃣ **Chew Ben Ben (Director)**

---

### Offence

Bribe:

**RM321,350**

Paid to:

COO of **Deleum Primera Sdn Bhd**

Purpose:

To secure a subcontract from **Petronas Carigali**.

---

# 1️⃣4️⃣ Foreign Case Example – Skansen IL

This case explains **adequate procedures defence**.

Company:  
Skansen IL (UK refurbishment company)

Managing director:  
Stephen Banks

---

### Bribery

Payments:

- £10,000
- £29,000

To secure:

Contracts worth **£6 million**.

---

### Court decision

The company **failed the adequate procedures defence** because:

They did NOT have:

1️⃣ Anti-bribery policy  
2️⃣ Compliance officer  
3️⃣ Employee training on anti-bribery laws

---

### Important lesson

Even though the company:

- reported the corruption
- cooperated with police

It was still convicted because:

👉 prevention measures were inadequate.

# 🧩 Quiz 

In the case of Skansen Interiors Limited, why did the jury reject the company's defense of having 'adequate procedures' despite having anti-bribery clauses in their contracts? :: <mark class="hltr-myyellow">They lacked a dedicated compliance officer and specific employee training</mark>


If a Malaysian company commits a bribery offence through an associated person while conducting business in a foreign country, it can still be prosecuted under Section 17A of the MACC Act 2009.
?
True ✅
> The definition of a 'commercial organisation' under Section 17A includes companies incorporated under the Companies Act 2016 and partnerships registered under the Partnership Act 1961, regardless of whether they are conducting business in Malaysia or elsewhere. This gives the provision extraterritorial reach for Malaysian-incorporated entities.


Under the T.R.U.S.T. principles, 'due diligence' is a reactive measure performed only after a report of corruption has been made through official reporting channels.
?
False ❌
> Due diligence is actually part of Principle 3 (Undertake control measures) and is a proactive measure. It involves background checks on projects, contractors, and new employees before they are appointed to reduce the risk of corruption, rather than being a reactive response to a report.


Under Section 17A of the MACC Act 2009, a {{<mark class="hltr-myyellow">Commercial Company</mark>}} is defined as a company or partnership that is registered in Malaysia or a foreign entity that carries out business within Malaysia.
