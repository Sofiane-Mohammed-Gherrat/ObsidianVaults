# 1. Business Messages

Business communication messages are usually classified into:

1. **Good news messages**

2. **Bad news messages**    

The **approach used depends on the type of message**.

---

# 2. Direct Approach

### Definition

The **direct approach** means:

> ➡️ **State the main idea immediately at the beginning of the message.**

The reader sees the <mark class="hltr-myyellow">result or decision first</mark>.

---

### Structure of Direct Approach

1. **Main idea / good news first**

2. **Explanation or details**

3. **Friendly closing**

---

### Used For

> The <mark class="hltr-mygreen2">direct</mark> approach is used when the message is <mark class="hltr-myyellow">positive or neutral</mark>.

Examples:

- Job acceptance

- Order confirmation

- Promotion announcements

- Request approval

- Routine business communication


---

### Advantages of Direct Approach

- Saves time
    
- Clear and efficient
    
- Reader understands message quickly
    
- Builds positive tone

---

# 3. Good News Messages

### Definition

> Messages that **make the reader happy or satisfied**.

Examples:

- Job offer
    
- Promotion
    
- Refund approval
    
- Successful application
    
- Order approval

---

### Structure of Good News Messages

1️⃣ **Start with the good news**  
2️⃣ Provide **supporting details**  
3️⃣ End with **friendly closing**

Example idea:

<mark class="hltr-mygreen2">Good news</mark> → <mark class="hltr-mygreen2">explanation</mark> → <mark class="hltr-mygreen2">closing</mark>.

---

### Key Principle

> **Be positive, clear, and friendly.**

---

# 4. Indirect Approach

### Definition

The **indirect approach** means:

> ➡️ **Do NOT give the bad news immediately.**

Instead:

1. Start with a **buffer**

2. Provide **reasons**

3. Then give the **bad news**


---

# 5. When to Use the Indirect Approach

Used when delivering:

- Bad news

- Negative decisions

- Rejections

- Complaints

- Sensitive information


<u>Purpose:</u>  

> ➡️ **Reduce negative reaction from the reader**

---

?
Typical order:
1️⃣ **Buffer**  
2️⃣ **Explanation / reasons**  
3️⃣ **Bad news**  
4️⃣ **Positive closing**

---

# 7. Buffer

### Definition

> A **neutral opening statement** that <mark class="hltr-mygreen2">prepares the reader</mark>.

Purpose:

- Avoid shock

- Maintain good relationship

- Lead into explanation


Example idea:

Instead of starting with rejection, start with appreciation.

---

# 8. Explanation (Reasons)

Provide **logical reasons before the bad news**.

Purpose:

- Help reader understand the decision
- Reduce disappointment


Important rule:  
> ➡️ Reasons must be <mark class="hltr-myyellow">clear and fair</mark>.

---

# 9. Delivering the Bad News

When writing the bad news:

- Be <mark class="hltr-myyellow">polite</mark>

- Be <mark class="hltr-myyellow">clear</mark> but <mark class="hltr-myyellow">not harsh</mark>

- Avoid <mark class="hltr-myyellow">blaming</mark> language

- Avoid overly <mark class="hltr-myyellow">negative</mark> tone


---

# 10. Closing in Bad News Messages

End with something **positive or forward-looking**.

Examples:

- Suggest alternatives

- Encourage future interaction

- Maintain goodwill


<font color="#fac08f">Purpose:</font>   

> ➡️ Preserve professional relationship.

---

# 11. Tone in Business Communication

Important characteristics:

- Professional

- Polite

- Respectful

- Clear


> Even in bad news messages, tone should remain **professional and considerate**.

---

# 12. Key Difference: Direct vs Indirect Approach

|Direct Approach|Indirect Approach|
|---|---|
|Main idea first|Main idea later|
|Used for good news|Used for bad news|
|Straightforward|More careful|
|Saves time|Protects reader feelings|

# 📝 From Gemini 


## **1. Routine and Good News Messages (Direct Approach)**

Because good news is always welcome, these messages should typically use the **direct approach**.

- **Routine Request Strategy:**
    
    - **State your request up front:** Be specific about what you want and use a respectful tone (e.g., "please," "I would appreciate").
        
    - **Explain and justify:** Explain the importance of the request or the benefits of responding.
        
    - **Ask important questions first:** Break complex requests into separate, single-topic questions.
        
    - **Courteous close:** Include contact info, express gratitude, and clearly state deadlines.
        
- **Three Basic Elements of Routine Requests:**
    
    1. What you want the reader to do.
        
    2. Why you are making the request (not always required).
        
    3. Why it is in the reader's interest to help (not always applicable).
        
- **Common Types of Routine Messages:**
    
    - Asking for information or action.
        
    - Asking for recommendations (e.g., for jobs, scholarships, or credit).
        
    - Making claims and requesting adjustments (formal complaints/settlements).


## **2. Bad News Messages (Direct vs. Indirect Approaches)**

The primary goals when communicating negative information are to convey the news, gain acceptance, maintain goodwill, and protect the organization's image.

- **Choosing the Approach:**
    
    - **Direct Approach:** Use for emergencies, minor/routine scenarios, or if the recipient prefers a direct style.
        
        - _Advantage:_ Creates a shorter message and reaches the main idea faster.
	
    - **Indirect Approach:** Use if the news will come as a shock or if the reader has a high emotional investment. This approach uses a **buffer** to help prepare the reader.
    
- **Writing Principles:**
    
    - **Tone:** The message must be clear and kind.
        
    - **Word Choice:** Use positive words rather than negative ones and embed negative statements within positive contexts.
        
    - **Accuracy:** Pay close attention to detail; audiences focus more on errors when reading negative news.
        

## **3. Goodwill Messages**

These are written specifically to build and foster positive relationships with no direct business purpose.

- **Congratulations:** Sent for business achievements (promotions, sales milestones) or personal highlights (weddings, births, graduations).
    
- **Appreciation:** Recognizes the contributions of others and encourages further excellence.
    
- **Condolences:** Brief personal messages to comfort someone after a death.
    
    - **Three Principles:** They should be **short, simple, and sincere**.
        

## **Quiz Focus Areas:**

- **When to use which approach:** Remember that "emergency" or "minor" = **Direct**, while "shocking" or "emotional investment" = **Indirect**.
    
- **Buffer:** This is the key distinguishing feature of the **Indirect approach**.
    
- **Tone and Detail:** Note that routine messages assume compliance, while bad news messages require extra care for errors because readers are more critical of them.
    
- **Condolence Principles:** Be ready to identify the three pillars: short, simple, and sincere



> However, you will encounter some situations i n which explaining negative news is neither appropriate nor helpful, such a s when the reasons are confidential, excessively complicated, or irrelevant t o the reader.

# 🟡 From Perplexity 

## Good News Messages

Good news uses the **direct approach** since it's welcome: start with the main idea, provide details, and end courteously. Key elements for routine requests include stating the request upfront (be specific, polite, assume compliance), explaining/justifying it (benefits, important questions first), and closing with specific action (deadlines, thanks, contact info). Goodwill messages (congratulations, appreciation, condolences) foster relationships—be sincere, specific, short/simple for condolences.
## Bad News Strategy Goals

Aim to convey the news, gain acceptance, maintain goodwill/image, and minimize future correspondence. Planning considers audience reluctance; writing uses clear/kind/positive words; completing checks for errors and prompt delivery.

## Direct Approach (Bad News)

Open with clear bad news statement (shortens message, quick to main point), follow with reasons/info (if appropriate, not confidential), close respectfully (alternatives if possible, no false hope). Use when: emergency, direct-preferring recipient, minor/routine news.
## Indirect Approach (Bad News)

**Buffer** first (neutral, sincere, relevant—e.g., appreciation; avoid misleading/flattery), then reasons (logical, positive focus, prepare for news), state bad news clearly but deemphasized (compound sentence middle, conditional if apt, what you _can_ do), close respectfully (no uncertainty/false optimism, sincere). Higher emotional impact; use for shocking/unexpected news, emotional investment.
## Bad News Types

Common types: rejecting proposals/suggestions (indirect, explain), transaction issues (modify expectations, resolve, repair relations), refusing claims (indirect, no blame, rational explain), negative employment (indirect, medium choice matters), social media negatives (engage/monitor/evaluate/respond). Focus on indirect preference for emotional/unexpected cases.

## Quiz Focus Areas

- **Approach choice**: Direct for good/minor (get attention fast); indirect for bad/shocking (cushion via buffer/reasons)

- **Buffers**: Good (relevant/sincere); bad (trivializes/misleads).

- **Deemphasis techniques**: Sentence position, conditionals, positives over negatives
